Executive Summary
Overview
What happened

Following joint US-Israeli strikes on Iran on February 28, the Strait of Hormuz, which normally handles 153+ vessel transits per day, collapsed to just 13 by March 1st and 3 by March 7th. The Joint Maritime Information Center confirmed just one inbound and one outbound crossing. P&I war-risk cancellations took effect March 5th. Carriers have imposed $800 to $4,000+ per-container surcharges, extending these to cargo already afloat and not yet discharged. Energy pricing, automotive production, and cold-chain freight are all showing measurable impact.

Feb 28 – Apr 13
Situation Timeline
Day-by-day developments
Apr 13

The U.S. military said it will blockade all Iranian Gulf ports on Monday (April 13, 2026) at 2 p.m. GMT, effectively seizing control of maritime traffic in the critical Strait of Hormuz.

Apr 08

New tolls could add $1 a barrel to Hormuz oil, crude costs rise ~$1/barrel as $2M/VLCC informal toll gets formalized.

Apr 03

CMA CGM announcing May 1 peak season surcharges on shipments from Asia to North America: $1,800 per 20-foot; $2,000 per 40-foot, and $2,530 per 45-foot containers.

Apr 03

A French-operated container ship became the first vessel of a major liner to exit the Strait of Hormuz since Iran asserted control. The 5,500-TEU CMA CGM Kribi crossed the narrow passage Thursday on its way out of the Persian Gulf.

Mar 29

Bahrain imposes curfew on all maritime activities. Nightly from 1800 to 0400, includes all vessels.

Mar 28

The Omani Port of Salalah came under attack on Saturday morning. Expect shipment delays up to 5 days and rerouting as port operations remain suspended following the drone strike.

Mar 27

Iran launches assault on Kuwait ports. First assault came at dawn on the Shuwaikh Port & a second attack was focused on the Mubarak Al-Kabeer Port in northern Kuwait.

Mar 25

As of March 25, the Strait of Hormuz remains functionally closed to liner container traffic. Carriers are slowly opening import and export bookings via Jeddah, Sohar, Fujairah, and Khor Fakkan. However, capacity can be limited.

Mar 24

Trump claimed that Iran wants to make a deal and the two countries have "major points of agreement". Iran has denied that any discussions with the US have taken place, insisting that its position on the Strait of Hormuz has not changed.

Mar 19

Iran strikes wipe out 17% of Qatar's LNG capacity, repairs could take 3–5 years.

Mar 17

The shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz has led to increased bunker prices, affecting global shipping costs.

Mar 16

LTL services in and out of Bahrain are currently suspended. FTL services are available. Containers are being discharged at Sohar and Salalah in Oman, Khor Fakkan in the UAE, Colombo in Sri Lanka, and Mundra in India.

Mar 16

The Strait of Hormuz remains functionally closed to liner container traffic, with only sporadic, non-liner or escorted movements reported; container carriers continue to treat the strait as non-navigable for scheduled services.

Mar 12

Maersk: 10 Vessels Stranded in Persian Gulf. As of March 12, Maersk confirmed 10 of its vessels are stranded inside the Persian Gulf, unable to exit through Hormuz. The company has not provided a timeline for resolution and is assessing diversion options on a vessel-by-vessel basis.

Mar 7

Only 3 total crossings recorded through the strait. GPS jamming affects 1,650+ vessels across the Gulf. IRGC strikes tanker PRIMA and tug MUSAFFAH 2, which later sinks. Iran reconfirms closure to Western-allied shipping on March 8th.

Mar 5

P&I and war-risk cancellations take effect. IRGC clarifies closure applies to US, Israeli and Western-allied vessels. Iran signals China-flagged vessels may be permitted selectively.

Mar 2–3

Carriers extend war-risk surcharges to cargo already on the water not yet discharged, a significant escalation in financial exposure for shippers.

Mar 1

Jebel Ali suspended. A fire breaks out inside the port following debris from an intercepted aerial threat. DP World suspends all operations across Terminals 1 to 4. Vessels are diverted to Khor Fakkan, Sharjah's enclave port on the Gulf of Oman side, accessible without a Hormuz transit.

Mar 1–2

Traffic collapses. A senior IRGC official officially confirms the strait is closed on March 2. Total transits fall to 13 on March 1 (down from 105 on Feb 28). Major carriers — Maersk, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd, MSC and COSCO — suspend transits and new bookings.

Feb 28

US and Israel launch coordinated strikes on Iran. Iran's IRGC announces retaliatory closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Outbound traffic that evening is heavy; inbound light. Three tankers are struck near the strait.

Sector Impacts
Industry analysis
Where the disruption is hitting hardest
Energy

Oil & LNG

Hormuz carries approximately 20% of the world's daily oil supply and ~20% of global LNG trade. Asian spot LNG (JKM) peaked at $25.39/MMBtu on March 3rd. European natural gas prices spiked up to 70% in the first week of March.

Automotive

Vehicle production

Toyota Motor is producing ~40,000 fewer vehicles for Middle East markets across March to April, representing roughly 60 to 70% of its normal monthly export volume there. Models affected include the Land Cruiser, sedans, and commercial vans.

Retail

Ocean & air freight

Capacity tightening and transit variability spreading across both ocean and air networks. Air cargo capacity across Gulf corridors has dropped sharply, with airspace closures forcing reroutes. Spot rate pressure mounting across non-Gulf trade lanes.

Food / Pharma

Cold chain

Higher reefer surcharges combined with diversion risk are increasing dwell times and cold-chain complexity. Spoilage exposure is elevated. Some food commodities, including sugar and fertiliser, are being selectively permitted through the strait.

Recommended actions
Routing Saudi Red Sea port routing

Saudi Red Sea ports are being used as alternative gateways for Gulf cargo as disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz limit direct Gulf vessel movements.

  • Re-evaluate routing for upcoming bookings to avoid potential delays in Gulf ports directly affected by Hormuz instability.
  • Consider routing Gulf-bound cargo via Saudi Red Sea ports (e.g., Jeddah, Yanbu) to bypass Hormuz disruptions.
  • Check with carriers for services using these Red Sea gateways.
  • Use feeder connections from Saudi ports to move cargo into Gulf markets.
Storage Free storage window (Hapag-Lloyd)

Shippers can leverage free or extended storage up to 10 days (notably from Hapag-Lloyd) to hold Gulf-bound cargo without additional cost.

  • Check if your shipments are with Hapag-Lloyd and confirm free-time eligibility.
  • Use the free window to delay cargo decisions without incurring storage costs.
  • Re-evaluate routing options (Red Sea / land bridge) while cargo is held.
  • Track expiry of free storage to avoid sudden charges.
Multimodal CMA CGM land-bridge routes

Expect alternative routing via land bridges to keep Gulf cargo moving, with slightly longer transit times but improved continuity.

  • Shift critical Gulf cargo to CMA CGM's land-bridge routes to avoid Hormuz disruption risk.
  • Prioritize time-sensitive shipments on these routes for better reliability vs waiting vessels.
  • Ask carriers for multimodal options (sea + land) to maintain supply continuity.
  • Use this as leverage to negotiate rates or service priority.
Capacity COSCO resumed bookings

Cosco has resumed bookings to the Middle East, bypassing the Strait of Hormuz due to ongoing tensions.

  • Book early with COSCO to secure space before demand and rates increase.
  • Prioritize critical shipments while capacity is temporarily available.
  • Confirm routing and ETAs as services resume but risks remain.
